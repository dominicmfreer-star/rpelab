The RPE chart, explained — and when not to trust it

The RPE chart is the most-shared, least-understood image in strength training. It maps a rep/RPE combo to a percentage of your 1RM — so "5 reps at RPE 8" tells you to load about 81% of your max, no calculator needed. Useful, but only if you actually understand what it's showing you and where it lies. Here's the honest guide.

What the numbers mean

Read the chart like a lookup table: find the row for your target RPE, follow it across to the column for your target reps, and the cell shows what percentage of your 1RM should let you hit that. It's built from a simple idea — every rep taken has a "cost," and total reps to failure predicts intensity fairly well within the range the chart covers.

How to read it in the gym

  1. Program says: "Top set of 4 @ RPE 8, then backoffs."
  2. Look up: row RPE 8, column 4 reps → about 84%.
  3. Do the math: if your 1RM is 400 lb, that's 336 lb — round to 335.
  4. Adjust to reality: take the set. If it comes in at RPE 8, great. If it feels like 9, your true 1RM is lower than 400 — reduce future top sets by ~5 lb until they land right.

The chart is a starting point, not a verdict. If you'd rather skip the lookup, our calculator does it live from any recent set.

Where the chart is accurate enough to trust

  • Reps 1–8, RPE 7–10 on compound lifts. This is the box the chart was built inside. Squat, bench, deadlift top sets and hard backoff work — the numbers hold up well here.
  • Established intermediates with recent 1RM data. Someone who tested a real max in the last 6–8 weeks will find the chart's predictions land within a few pounds most of the time.
  • Off-season and volume phases where you're not chasing a specific date and small daily variance doesn't hurt you.

Three situations where the chart lies

1. Rep ranges above 10

The chart's math breaks down at higher rep counts. A "set of 15 @ RPE 8" is a fatigue soup: cardio, grip, mental willingness, and lactic tolerance all matter more than pure strength. Two lifters at the same 1RM can hit wildly different loads at that rep range. Above about 10–12 reps, treat the chart as directional only.

2. Very high or very low RPEs on unfamiliar lifts

RPE calibration is lift-specific. A trained lifter can rate their squat accurately but be a full point off on a new variation — safety squat bar, deficit deadlift, incline bench. The chart's percentages assume accurate rating; if your rating is off by a point, the load is off by 3–5%.

3. Peaks, meet week, and single-rep testing

Chart math is smoothed averages. On a real max attempt, nervous system state, adrenaline, warmup quality, and attempt selection dominate — the chart's "97.8% for a single at RPE 9.5" is a rough guideline, not a prescription. Coaches program singles from feel and prior performance during a peak, not from a lookup table.

What the chart doesn't try to do

The chart is a load estimator, not a program. It won't tell you how many sets to do, how long to rest, when to deload, or whether to add weight next session. Those are programming decisions — the chart just tells you what today's load should be once you've made them.

It also doesn't account for lift-to-lift differences in how much your reserve is worth. RPE 9 on a bench is one rep in the tank; RPE 9 on a deadlift often means one grinder in the tank that will cost you the next session. The chart treats them the same. Coaches don't.

In your next session

Pick your main lift. Program a top set at RPE 8 for a rep target you can rate honestly (3–6 reps works best). Look up the percentage on the chart, calculate the load, take the set, and record what it actually felt like. Do this for two weeks. You'll quickly see whether the chart's numbers land on your body or if you need to calibrate your 1RM. That feedback loop is the entire point — the chart isn't a rule, it's a hypothesis you test.

Common questions

Are all RPE charts the same?

Close, but not identical. The RTS/Tuchscherer chart is the most-cited, and other versions (Sheiko-derived, various coaches') differ by 1–2% in most cells. For practical training, treat them as interchangeable.

Should I memorize the chart?

No. Memorize a few anchor points that show up in your program most: RPE 8 for 3, 5, and 8 reps. That covers 90% of your top sets. Look up the rest.

What if my chart lookup and my actual set disagree?

Trust the set. The chart is an average across lifters; your body is a single data point. When the two disagree consistently, your true 1RM has moved — test it or recalibrate downward.
