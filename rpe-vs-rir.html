RPE vs RIR: the difference, and why it matters less than you think

You'll see both terms in programs, and coaches sometimes switch between them mid-block as if they're two different tools. They're not. RPE and RIR are the same measurement pointing in opposite directions — and choosing between them is really about which question your brain answers faster.

The exact conversion

RIR = 10 − RPE. That's the whole formula. In practice:

  • RPE 10 = 0 reps in reserve (RIR 0)
  • RPE 9 = 1 RIR
  • RPE 8 = 2 RIR
  • RPE 7 = 3 RIR
  • RPE 6 = 4 RIR

Half points work the same. RPE 8.5 = 1.5 RIR. Nothing else to know.

Where they came from

RPE started in cardiovascular research in the 1960s — Borg's original scale ran 6 to 20 to loosely map heart rate. Mike Tuchscherer adapted it to strength training in the late 2000s as a 1–10 scale focused on reps left in the tank. RIR is essentially the same idea named more literally: instead of asking "how hard was that," it asks "how many were left." Bodybuilding communities and hypertrophy researchers gravitated to RIR because the question felt more mechanical and less subjective.

Which one to use — the honest answer

Use whichever your brain answers faster. That's it. There's no meaningful physiological difference; there's a real cognitive one.

  • RPE readers tend to be lifters who track effort holistically — they feel the set as a whole and rate it against internal memory of past sets.
  • RIR readers tend to be lifters who count — they finish a set and immediately visualize doing one more rep, then another, until they hit the wall in their head.

Neither is "more accurate" in a lab setting. Studies comparing them find near-identical predictive value at the same score. Pick the one that snaps to an answer for you within two seconds of racking the bar. If both feel fuzzy, stick with RPE and calibrate — the field guide covers how to sharpen ratings in three weeks.

Where you'll see each in the wild

  • Powerlifting programs almost universally use RPE. The 1–10 scale reads faster in a program document ("top set @ 8, backoffs at 7.5" beats "top set at 2 RIR, backoffs at 2.5").
  • Hypertrophy programs often use RIR. Renaissance Periodization popularized it in bodybuilding, and the RIR framing pairs well with counting proximity to failure across many sets.
  • Coach-written custom programs mix freely — you'll see "sets of 8-10 @ RPE 8" and "3 x 12 at 1-2 RIR" in the same document. Convert whichever way is faster.

The one time the distinction matters

At very high effort — RPE 9.5 to 10, or RIR 0.5 to 0 — the two framings can pull ratings in different directions. Some lifters underrate an RPE 10 (calling it 9 because "it wasn't quite max") but accurately report RIR 0. The question "how many were left" grounds the rating in a concrete number, while "how hard was that" invites hedging. If your ratings feel soft at the top end of the scale, try switching to RIR just for max-effort sets. That's the only edge case where switching formats changes anything.

In your next session

Pick one format. Log it after every working set for a full week. If ratings feel automatic and you can defend each one, keep going. If you're second-guessing every number, try the other format for a week. Whichever wins that A/B test is yours — don't switch mid-program after that.

Common questions

Does research prefer RPE or RIR?

Neither. Studies comparing them show near-identical accuracy when lifters are trained on the scale they're using. The variable that matters is calibration, not format.

Can I use both in the same program?

Yes, but there's no reason to. Pick the one that comes to mind fastest and stick with it. Mixing formats mid-session just adds mental overhead without adding information.

What's a "0.5 RIR" set actually like?

The rep you just did was your last clean rep. If you'd tried one more, you'd have failed it or grinded through it with form breakdown. It's the RPE 9.5 answer in different words.
