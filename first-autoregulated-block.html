Your first auto-regulated training block, step by step

Every guide to autoregulation stops at "run RPE targets and let the day decide." That's not a program — that's a slogan. This is the actual first block: four weeks of structure, exact RPE targets, a deload, and how to read the data at the end so week five is a real decision instead of a guess. Written for intermediate lifters running their first honest RPE block.

Before you start

Two things need to be true, or this block will teach you the wrong lessons:

  1. You can rate within about half a point of reality. If your RPE ratings are still noisy, the block runs on bad data. Spend three weeks calibrating first — the field guide walks through it.
  2. You have a recent 1RM estimate. Not a chest-thumping fantasy — a real number, tested in the last 8 weeks or reliably estimated from a hard set. If you're not sure, take an honest set of 3–5 reps and use the calculator to convert.

If either is missing, fix it first. This block assumes both.

The block, in one paragraph

Four sessions per week. Two main lifts per session — one heavy, one moderate. Top set at a target RPE, followed by three backoff sets at 10% less. Same lift rotation every week, same rep targets, RPE climbing by half a point per week for three weeks, then a deload in week four. That's it. The complexity is in the execution, not the structure.

The four-day split

  • Monday: Squat (heavy) · Bench (moderate) · accessories
  • Tuesday: Deadlift (heavy) · Overhead press (moderate) · accessories
  • Thursday: Bench (heavy) · Squat (moderate) · accessories
  • Friday: Overhead press (heavy) · Deadlift (moderate) · accessories

Each session hits one lift as the main event and another as a maintenance stimulus. If you can't train four days, drop to three by cutting Friday and moving overhead press onto Thursday as the moderate movement. Accessories are whatever fits your body — chin-ups, rows, curls, calves. Not the focus.

The main-lift progression

This is the block's engine. Every heavy main-lift day looks the same shape — top set at target RPE, three backoffs at 10% less — with the target RPE climbing weekly:

  • Week 1: Top set of 5 @ RPE 7 · 3 backoffs at ~10% less
  • Week 2: Top set of 4 @ RPE 8 · 3 backoffs at ~10% less
  • Week 3: Top set of 3 @ RPE 9 · 3 backoffs at ~10% less
  • Week 4 (deload): Top set of 5 @ RPE 6 · 2 backoffs at ~10% less

Moderate-lift days use the same rep target as the heavy day for that lift, but capped at RPE 7 all four weeks. Volume without accumulating fatigue. On deload week, cap moderate work at RPE 5.

How to pick the actual weight

You have two options. Both work.

Option 1 — By feel, warmup up: work up in small jumps, rating each warmup. When a warmup lands at about RPE 6, you're close to your target. Take the next jump and see. If you overshoot RPE, drop the load 5–10 lb and take it again. If you undershoot, add and go. Log what the top set actually was.

Option 2 — Calculate first, adjust to reality: use the calculator to convert your current 1RM into today's target load. Warm up, take the set, log what it actually rated. If it came in +0.5 RPE from target, drop the load for next week by 2%; -0.5, add 2%. The math self-corrects over the block.

Beginners to RPE do better with option 2 — it gives you a starting anchor before you have to trust your ratings.

What week 1 should feel like

Almost too easy. RPE 7 with three reps in the tank should feel like a warm working set, not a challenge. Most lifters running their first RPE block instinctively want to lift more — resist that. The purpose of week 1 is calibration and recovery loading, not stimulus. Week 3 is the hard one; save your bandwidth.

What week 3 should feel like

The top set is your true edge. RPE 9 means one rep in reserve on your best working set of the block. Backoffs at 10% less will feel harder than week 1 top sets — that's cumulative fatigue, and it's the entire point. If backoffs on week 3 don't feel meaningfully heavier than backoffs on week 1, you're leaving stimulus on the table.

The deload — how to actually deload

Week 4 is the trap most people fall into. They "deload" by doing 90% of week 3 and calling it recovery. That's not a deload; that's a slightly-lower training week.

A real deload: reduce top set to RPE 6 (four reps in the tank), cut backoff sets from three to two, keep the same rep target. Load will feel light. It should. Your job is to keep the movement pattern fresh while your recovery catches up. If the deload feels like training, redo it — you didn't back off enough.

Reading the block after week 4

End-of-block review is where autoregulation earns its keep over percentage-based programming. Look at your log and answer four questions:

  1. Did top-set loads climb week to week for each main lift? Yes = the block worked. No = calibration was off or fatigue outran recovery. Either is fixable.
  2. Were your ratings consistent within a lift? If Monday squats always rated 9 and Thursday's moderate squat felt like a 9 at 20% less, something's wrong with rating skill, not the program.
  3. Which lift improved most? That's your body telling you where the current stimulus fits. Keep that lift's structure next block.
  4. Which lift stalled or regressed? Not a failure — a signal. Consider changing rep range, adding a second weekly exposure, or checking whether you're rating that lift honestly.

Answers to these four questions become next block's program. That's autoregulation at the block scale — the day makes small decisions, the block makes bigger ones.

What to do next

Two options after week 4:

  • Repeat the block with adjusted starting loads (based on how week 3 landed) if the structure worked. Most intermediates get 3–4 productive blocks out of this template before needing to change the shape.
  • Retest your 1RM if week 3's top single rated below RPE 9 across two main lifts — your max has moved and the whole block was undercalibrated. Test, recalibrate, run it again.

Do not run this block for 12+ weeks straight without variation. The point of a block is that it ends.

In your next session

Print or save this article. Open your log. Write "Block 1, Week 1, Day 1" at the top. Pick a start date on a Monday you'll actually train. Do the work. Every rating matters; the log is the data set. In 28 days you'll have your first honest autoregulated block behind you and answers to the four questions above — real information, not vibes.

Common questions

Can I run this three days a week instead?

Yes. Cut Friday, move overhead press to Thursday as the moderate lift. Total weekly volume drops by roughly 20%, which is fine for anyone with high life stress or limited recovery.

What if I miss a session?

Skip it. Don't move it to a different day and don't make it up. The block assumes recovery windows between sessions — cramming them together defeats the point.

Should accessories be RPE-based too?

Not on your first block. Accessories at 2–3 sets of 8–15, stopping about 2 reps short of failure by feel. Adding formal RPE to accessories adds tracking overhead without much return on your first pass through the system.

What if week 3 breaks me?

Then week 4's deload wasn't optional advice — it was a rescue. Run it as written. If you're still fried after deload, week 3 was too heavy; drop starting loads by 5% for the next block.
